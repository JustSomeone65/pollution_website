Brief History

Nashville International Airport first opened in June 1937 and named Berry Field in honor of Colonel Harry S. Berry. Colonel Berry was the state administrator of the Works Progress Administration (WPA), who were responsible for the construction. When first built, Berry Field consisted of a terminal building, two hangers, a 4,000 feet concrete runway and a flashing beacon on top of a 340-acre site located along what is now Murfreesboro Road. The airportâ€™s three letter identifier, BNA, stands for Berry Field NAshville. During World War II, the airport became the military base for the 4th Ferrying Command, which saw the airport increase in size to 1,500 acres. The rapid growth of air transportation during the 1950s and 1960s led to the creation of a new terminal and crosswind runway, along with an expansion to the existing runway. By 1977, Berry Field had increased to 3,300 acres with 3 runways and a renovated terminal. Yet, the expansion of the airport continued during the 1980s, with the opening of JWN airport for small planes and the addition of a new terminal. In 1988, the airport's name, Berry Field, was replaced by Nashville International Airport. Nashville International Airport had other major terminal renovations in 2012, the same year that marked the 75th anniversary of the airport's opening. As of 2015, over 11 million passengers travel through the airport. The airport has daily arriving and departing flights to other parts of the United States, Canada, Mexico, Jamaica, and more recently England.
